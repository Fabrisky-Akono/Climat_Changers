The Rise of Civilization

As humans evolved from hunter-gatherers to agricultural societies, they began to alter their surroundings. They discovered fire for warmth and cooking, cultivated crops for food, and domesticated animals. With the advent of agriculture around 10,000 years ago, civilizations began to flourish. Fast forward to the Industrial Revolution in the 18th century: a groundbreaking era that transformed human society. Factories emerged, powered by coal and later oil. Transportation evolved with steam engines and automobiles. While these advancements improved living standards and fueled economic growth, they also marked the beginning of significant environmental impact. The Awakening

The Awakening

By the mid-20th century, scientists began to observe troubling signs. Global temperatures were steadily rising; glaciers in remote regions were melting at unprecedented rates. Reports indicated that extreme weather events—hurricanes, droughts, floods—were becoming more frequent and intense. In 1970, the first Earth Day was celebrated, marking a pivotal moment in environmental awareness. Activists rallied together to demand action against pollution and habitat destruction. The message was clear: human activities were altering the climate in ways that could have catastrophic consequences. A Call to Action

A call to Action

As awareness grew, so did public concern. In 1988, the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) was established to assess scientific information related to climate change. Their reports highlighted the urgent need for action and provided evidence that human activities were driving climate change. Grassroots movements sprang up worldwide. Activists like Greta Thunberg inspired millions with their passionate calls for climate action. Communities organized protests demanding governments take responsibility for reducing carbon emissions and transitioning to renewable energy sources.
